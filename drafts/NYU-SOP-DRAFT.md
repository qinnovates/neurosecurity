# Statement of Purpose: NYU MA in Bioethics

**Applicant:** Kevin Qi

---

I am a security engineer who spent fifteen years protecting systems, and then my own system failed. COVID isolation led to a B12 deficiency that caused temporary paralysis and loss of motor control. I could not walk for months. My hands were uncontrollable. I did not feel like myself. I am trained to detect exactly this kind of drift in systems, yet I could not detect it in my own body until it was already happening. Once you have felt your own nervous system fail you, the idea of plugging a machine into that system carries weight. The first time I came across Ienca and Andorno's neurorights framework, I was not reading about theoretical concepts. I was reading an account of my own life, and it made me ask what all of this means for a patient using a brain-computer interface to regain sight, movement or speech.

I started this work with one question: what happens to a patient when their brain-computer interface fails to protect them? Not what happens to the data, not what happens to the device, but what happens to the person. I spent fifteen years in cybersecurity, from biotech startups to Blackstone to Match Group, and in that time I learned that the internet was built without security and that BCIs are about to repeat the same mistake. So I built QIF, an open-source framework that catalogs 109 BCI attack techniques and maps each one to the neurorights it violates, backed by 193 verified sources with everything published and everything open. What surprised me most was that 77 of those techniques have direct therapeutic counterparts, meaning the same stimulation that causes involuntary movement in one context treats Parkinson's in another. The difference is not the mechanism but the consent, the dosage, and the oversight. When I read Beauchamp and Childress, I realized I had been mapping that tension between beneficence and nonmaleficence for years without having the language for it. I can build the threat map. I cannot build the ethics alone.

The question I keep coming back to is what meaningful consent looks like when the technology itself might change how the patient thinks. Respect for autonomy assumes a stable decision-maker. BCIs challenge that assumption at the hardware level. When a malfunction disrupts neural processing without the patient noticing — through gradual retuning or homeostatic drift — current neurorights frameworks have nothing to say, because they were designed to detect intrusion, not drift. I am living proof of that failure mode. The person is still in there when it happens, but the wiring is not carrying the signal cleanly. That is not a hypothetical. That is a real problem that needs philosophical grounding before these devices reach patients at scale, and it is the problem I want to work on at NYU.

I chose NYU's bioethics program because the questions I am asking are philosophical before they are technical. Dr. Liao has argued that human rights protect the fundamental conditions for pursuing a good life, including the ability to think, be motivated by facts, and choose and act freely. A brain-computer interface that malfunctions or is compromised can violate every single one of those conditions, and my framework catalogs exactly how that can happen. I want to bring QIF into his research on near-term neurotechnology ethics and test whether his human rights grounding can anchor the governance that these devices need. If a rights-based framework cannot account for threats to cognition itself, we need to know that now. Dr. Passos-Ferreira's research on consciousness raises a question my framework cannot answer on its own: if a BCI alters the neural correlates of conscious experience, at what point has it changed who the patient is? That question is at the center of my work on cognitive drift. I need a philosopher of mind to help me think through it properly. And the question I have not seen anyone else address is this: who watches the watcher when the thing being watched is the patient's own cognition? Dr. Sebo's Center for Mind, Ethics, and Policy is where that question lives. The moral status of a mind augmented by a machine is not something cybersecurity frameworks were built to answer.

What draws me to NYU is that the questions are philosophical first, but the program does not stop at philosophy. The neuroethics course covers mind-reading technologies and brain privacy, which are not abstractions for me but categories in my threat taxonomy. Cross-enrollment at NYU Law would let me study cognitive liberty and the legal frameworks for neural data, because the governance I want to build needs to hold up in court, not just in a seminar. The practicum gives me a chance to take this work out of the framework and into an organization where it can be tested against real institutional constraints. I want to work alongside peers from philosophy, law, and clinical ethics to take what QIF maps technically and translate it into language that ethics boards and regulators can act on. That translation is not something a security engineer can do alone. It is not something an ethicist should have to do without the technical layer underneath.

After this degree, I plan to lead the development of safety standards for neurotechnology while staying technical. Working directly with the FDA, IEEE, and WHO to translate technical risk into governance that clinicians and patients can trust. BCIs exist to help people get back what they lost. We have to get the ethics into the engineering before someone gets hurt, not after. That is why I am applying now, while the standards are still being written. I would rather help write them than spend my career patching what went wrong without them.
