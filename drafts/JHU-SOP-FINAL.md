# Statement of Purpose: Johns Hopkins Master of Bioethics (MBE)

**Applicant:** Kevin Qi

---

I am a security engineer who spent fifteen years protecting systems, and then my own system failed. COVID isolation led to a B12 deficiency that caused temporary paralysis and loss of motor control. I could not walk for months. My hands were uncontrollable. I did not feel like myself. I am trained to detect exactly this kind of drift in systems, yet I could not detect it in my own body until it was already happening. Once you have felt your own nervous system fail you, the idea of plugging a machine into that system carries weight. The first time I came across Ienca and Andorno's neurorights framework, I was not reading about theoretical concepts. I was reading an account of my own life, and it made me ask what all of this means for a patient using a brain-computer interface to regain sight, movement or speech.

I started this work with one question: what happens to a patient when their brain-computer interface fails to protect them? Not what happens to the data, not what happens to the device, but what happens to the person. I spent fifteen years in cybersecurity, from biotech startups to Blackstone to Match Group, and in that time I learned that the internet was built without security and that BCIs are about to repeat the same mistake. So I built QIF, an open-source framework that catalogs 109 BCI attack techniques and maps each one to the neurorights it violates, backed by 193 verified sources with everything published and everything open. What surprised me most was that 77 of those techniques have direct therapeutic counterparts, meaning the same stimulation that causes involuntary movement in one context treats Parkinson's in another. The difference is not the mechanism but the consent, the dosage, and the oversight. When I read Beauchamp and Childress, I realized I had been mapping that tension between beneficence and nonmaleficence for years without having the language for it, and I can build the threat map, but I cannot build the ethics alone.

During COVID, while serving as Wellness Champion at Tinder, the company announced it was cutting free unlimited telehealth therapy. I saw the distress on my team's faces. So I raised it. They reversed the decision. That was my first time making a moral argument in a corporate setting that was not about security policy but about people getting access to mental health care. The instinct was right, but instinct is not enough. I kept making moral judgments using cybersecurity norms instead of bioethics principles, and I knew that was not good enough. The most important safeguards for brain-computer interfaces will not be technical. They will be the policies, the consent frameworks, and the ethical guidelines that determine how these devices are developed, tested, and deployed. I want to help build those.

The question I keep coming back to is what meaningful consent looks like when the technology itself might change how the patient thinks. Respect for autonomy assumes a stable decision-maker. BCIs challenge that assumption at the hardware level. When a malfunction disrupts neural processing without the patient noticing — through gradual retuning or homeostatic drift — current neurorights frameworks have nothing to say, because they were designed to detect intrusion, not drift. I am living proof of that failure mode. The person is still in there when it happens, but the wiring is not carrying the signal cleanly. These are real problems that need answers before these devices reach patients at scale.

What I bring to the Berman Institute is something most MBE cohorts do not have walking in the door: a working threat taxonomy for an emerging technology that is about to need governance, and fifteen years of building security infrastructure at scale. What I need from the Berman Institute is everything I cannot teach myself. Dr. Mathews works at the intersection of bioethics and assured autonomy. I want to bring QIF into her research and let her challenge whether the threat model holds up when you put ethical weight on it. If it does not hold up in her seminar, it will not hold up in front of a regulatory body. Alan Regenberg studies the ethics of early human trials, and that is the piece I need most: when your data says there is risk, who gets to decide whether a trial moves forward? What do you owe the patient who volunteers anyway? I want to work alongside my MBE peers from philosophy, law, and clinical ethics to take what QIF maps technically and translate it into language that ethics boards and regulators can act on. That translation is not something a security engineer can do alone. It is not something an ethicist should have to do without the technical layer underneath. Hopkins is also building organoid intelligence with real neural tissue wired to silicon. If our cohort can test security controls on biological tissue before they ever touch a patient, that changes how we validate neural safeguards entirely. I have a preprint that needs people who will push back on it rather than agree with it. The question I have not seen anyone else address is this: who watches the watcher when the thing being watched is the patient's own cognition? I want to work through that with people who will take it apart properly.

After this degree, I plan to lead the development of safety standards for neurotechnology while staying technical. Working directly with the FDA, IEEE, and WHO to translate technical risk into governance that clinicians and patients can trust. BCIs exist to help people get back what they lost. We have to get the ethics into the engineering before someone gets hurt, not after. That is why I am applying now, while the standards are still being written. I would rather help write them than spend my career patching what went wrong without them.
