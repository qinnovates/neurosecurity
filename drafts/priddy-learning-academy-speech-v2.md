## 2. THE SHY KID [~1:15]
*[SERIOUS. Drop the comedy. This is the real story. Make it relatable.]*

Man, I'm such an unc now. It's been almost 30 years since I first sat in this building, you know, picking my nose and staring at words I couldn't read. I was digging my knowledge in my noggin!

*[smile and move to other side of stage]*

Don't laugh. It could be any of you up here next. [point]

30 years ago, we moved right across the street, in the apartments. I had no excuses to not make it. I couldn't go to the park to play even if I tried. They'd see me through the windows.

Here's the truth. Growing up, I had very low expectations for myself. My bar was set very low. I was not the best student.

I couldn't even hold a gaze with adults, let alone utter a single word of English. I was very, very shy. So shy I didn't say a single word in 1st or 2nd grade until the week of Christmas.

One girl screamed: "I THOUGHT YOU WERE MUTE. YOU SPEAK? ENGLISH?"

Maybe all the Magic School Bus and Priddy's Learning Academy must've done something to my little 7-year-old mind because I was formulating full sentences in English for the first time.

## 3. MOM, MAVIS BEACON, AND THE MIDNIGHT INTERNET [~1:30]
*[Warm. Nostalgic. Build the origin story.]*

My parents worked long hours bussing tables and washing dishes, so naturally, this place became my second home. Mr. Priddy and Fangjie like my family.

Mr. and Mrs. Priddy were smart. They caught on quick that I wouldn't finish my homework, so they had me sit through and finish them before I left. It was GRUEEEELING. But I needed it.

All I knew was video games and the internet was full of them. I remember being in a bookstore once and begging my mom for a video game.

I know what you're thinking: "A bookstore? You mean Amazon?"

*[pause for laughs]*

I tried to make a deal with her. Promising I'd read more if she got me the game. That motherly instinct quickly counter-offered with this professional-looking game sitting right next to it. "Mavis Beacon Teaches Typing."

She said okay, but: "First you play this once a week on the weekend. You'll need to learn it one day." This was when personal computers finally started being affordable and she read the market right.

Moms just know.

Surprisingly, that game made typing and learning fun. It was my first experience of gamified learning. I was young, I had no idea what I was doing, but the novelty of following the screen to type the keys like a grocery store version of Guitar Hero had me.

I never learned how to type the proper way. With your fingers over the home row keys like they teach in school. That didn't work for me. I type my own way. Faster than Gen Z gossiping at the mall.

*[pause]*

Little did I know. That was the first experience of me realizing that maybe everyone has their own way of learning and doing. There's no right or wrong if it works.

In all seriousness, have you ever seen someone fall asleep sitting up? Yeah, that was me. Every summer. I got very good at making it look like I was reading. Really, it was because I was staying up late sneaking on the family computer in the middle of the night without my parents knowing.

Come on kids. Your parents aren't that dumb. They know when you try to secretly play with the iPad when you're not supposed to.

*[point]* You know who you are. Don't look like you never thought about it.

*[pause for laughs]*

## 4. STRANGER TEACHER [~1:00]
*[The internet origin story. Keep it tight.]*

The irony is, those video game days led me to meeting people online who shared the same interests. I remember meeting someone named Aaron who was older than me on some video game community.

What could've been stranger danger became stranger teacher.

Next thing you know we're using AOL Instant Messenger coming up with the most diabolical plans to create a web community showing how to make complex designs simple ways. Not because that was some genius plan. We were just two kids who only knew how to replicate complex designs given the limited knowledge we both had.

Our plan? Pump out as many Photoshop tutorials as we could, plaster them across every site, and link it all back to the forum community we made.

It worked. In a year or two, we had 3,000 registered members. I was 12.

I didn't realize until years later that this was Aaron's plan to build a portfolio for college. He just pulled me along because he noticed my work once. Did my parents know I was doing this? No.

*[glance at mom]*

Sorry, Mom.

## 5. THE WAKE-UP CALL [~1:00]
*[Vulnerable. Real. No performance.]*

I wasn't the best student. I failed Calculus I in college. Twice. And when I say failed, I don't mean I almost passed. I mean I showed up confused and left more confused. I thought that meant I wasn't a math person. That there was some ceiling on what my brain could do, and I had hit it.

My GPA went from a 1.7 to a 4.0. But that turnaround didn't happen by accident. It got so bad that if I was put on one more academic probation, I would've lost my financial aid and been expelled. Yeah, that was a wake-up call.

*[glance at mom again]*

Oops. I never told my mom.

*[pause for laughs]*

What changed was finding something that actually lit me up. I switched my focus, found my lane, and the grades followed. It wasn't that I couldn't learn. It was that the way I was being taught didn't match the way my brain works.

Maybe the normal curriculum wasn't tailored for me. What I do know is that I learned the very frameworks for life here, and I only excelled because of teachers who recognize that learning is more than just a curriculum. It starts with identifying what's unique in each one of us.

## 6. WHAT IT LED TO [~1:00]
*[Career arc. Quick beats. Not a resume, a through-line.]*

So what happened to the shy kid who couldn't speak English?

I went on to study IT at Rutgers. I chartered a fraternity chapter that was about to disband (first in history to allow Jewish and Christians back in 1889). I said no, not if I can help it, and I recruited 12 people that semester. Same instinct. See something broken, refuse to let it stay broken.

That instinct carried me into IT. My first job, the entire team quit. 100% turnover except me, the part-timer. I had to learn everything fast and hold the whole thing together alone. Trial by fire.

From there I went on to build security systems at some of the largest companies in the world. Financial institutions. A global investment firm where I helped protect the digital footprint of senior leadership. A tech company you've probably swiped on.

*[pause — let them figure it out]*

At that last one, I built the security standard that got adopted across 40+ brands worldwide. Trained every team on it. When it was done, my chapter there was done.

And now? Now I study how to protect the human brain from cyberattacks. Brain-computer interfaces. Devices that connect silicon to neurons. I'm building the security framework for that. And I'm heading to graduate school for neuroethics.

I didn't see that coming. You won't see yours coming either.

## 7. THE PI CHALLENGE [~30s]
*[Callback to Priddy. Emotional beat.]*

Mrs. Priddy, you might remember the pi challenge you gave me. I never told you then, but I memorized 13 digits. 3.1415926-53589.

That blew my own mind. Not because it was impressive. Because it told me something I didn't believe yet: that I could do hard things.

Fun fact: Miller's Law. No, not Millburn Miller's. Psychologist George Miller. He found that our working memory holds about 5 to 9 items, 7 being the median. That's why phone numbers are the length they are. I beat the median. On a dare.

You can too. It's just a matter of understanding how your mind works differently. The tools are all the same. The approach is yours. These are the frameworks you're building today. The things you think you're learning now that may be irrelevant, you'll find a day when it clicks. You'll think back to this moment.

---

## 8. AI AND THE LEGO PILE [~1:30]
*[The thesis. Pace back and forth like Jobs.]*

When I failed Calculus, I didn't understand it because I was staring at flat pictures in a textbook trying to imagine 3D shapes. A decade later, I went back and built my own visualizations. I used AI to help me see what the textbook couldn't show me.
At least I didn't give up, I tried again and failed again! Hah.

Suddenly, it clicked. Not because AI gave me the answer. Because AI helped me see the problem differently. And then my brain did the work.

That's the difference. AI is a tool. Like a hammer. A hammer can help you build a house, but it can't imagine the house for you. If you let AI do your thinking, you're handing someone else your Legos and asking them to build your dream. And they'll build theirs.

There's a quote I love. Neil deGrasse Tyson said something like: what's dangerous isn't what you don't know. It's what you think you know that just isn't so.

AI can sound very smart and be completely wrong. It can write a perfect-sounding essay that says nothing real. It can solve a math problem with the wrong method and get the right answer by accident. If you don't know enough to check it, you won't catch it. Your wisdom is the guardrail. Without it, you're just trusting a very confident stranger.

The thing is, if I didn't have the critical thinking and analytical frameworks I learned here, would I have thought to look at that Calculus problem from a completely different angle? I'm not sure. But I know where those frameworks started. In this room.

## 9. THE HIDDEN TALENT + CLOSE [~1:00]
*[Bring it home. Allocentric. About THEM.]*

So here's what I actually came to tell you.

Learning is like a pile of Legos. It comes in all different sizes, shapes, and forms. We've all stubbed our toes on a brick or two. Painful in the moment. But that pain is just a reminder that an entire pile is sitting there, waiting to be built into something.

Here's the beauty about Legos. Not all of them come with instructions for a reason.

Some of you are going to get the instruction set. The clear path. The A to B to C. That's great. Follow it. But some of you are going to get a pile. No box. No picture on the front. Just pieces. And you're going to have to look at that pile and imagine what it could become.

Both paths build something real. Neither one is wrong.

I got the pile. The Priddys helped me see that the pieces were there.

You have something. Every single one of you. It might not look like what school rewards. It might not show up on a test. Mine didn't. Mine showed up in Photoshop tutorials at midnight and memorizing 13 digits of pi because someone dared me to try.

Your job isn't to find the instructions. Your job is to look at your pile and dream how you wish. You're going to stub your toes. You're going to fail Calculus. Maybe twice. You're going to fall asleep in rooms where you're supposed to be paying attention.

But one day you're going to look back and realize that every single one of those pieces, even the ones that hurt, was part of what you built. Moving pieces and rebuilding is all part of the game.

There's a reason why not all Legos come with instructions.
It's modular and will always fit if you allow it.

Just don't forget, the figurines!

*[pause]*

I challenge you. Design the build and be mindful of which mountain you pick. If you ever feel stuck or uncertain, seek from within and confide in others. You'd be surprised how many people have gone through similar challenges, just different stories.

Like the formula for learning anything, it starts logarithmic. It's like hiking a mountain you've never explored. Your brain will naturally tell you to go do something easier. That's just the amygdala. The monkey mind. Fight or flight kicking in. Just fight it. If you really want it bad enough, you'll get it.

I'm still at the beginning of my climb right now. Just keep pushing.

*[turn to Priddys]*

Mr. Priddy, Fangjie. Thank you for gathering us all here today. I really don't think you'll ever know the sheer magnitude of impact you and the instructors at Priddy's Learning Academy have made on all of us.

Now go out there and find what moves your Legos.

Thank you.
