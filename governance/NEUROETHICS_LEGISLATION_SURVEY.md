## 1. Enacted National Legislation

### 1.1 Chile — Constitutional Amendment (Ley 21.383)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Full name** | Ley N° 21.383 — Constitutional Amendment to Article 19 No. 1 of the Political Constitution |
| **Date enacted** | Promulgated October 14, 2021; published and in force October 25, 2021 |
| **Scope** | Constitutional (supreme law) |
| **Status** | **Enacted** — world's first constitutional neurorights protection |
| **Key provisions** | Amends Art. 19 No. 1 to protect "brain activity" and "mental integrity" against technological interference. Mental privacy, free will, non-discrimination in neurotechnology access. Scientific/technological development must serve people. |
| **Implementing statute** | Bulletin 13.828-19 (operationalizes the amendment for private neurotech actors) — under legislative processing; passage status unconfirmed as of March 2026 |
| **Landmark case** | Chilean Supreme Court Ruling Rol N° 1.080-2020 (August 2023) — ordered Emotiv Inc. to delete neural data. World's first Supreme Court neurorights decision. |
| **URL** | [Carey Abogados summary](https://www.carey.cl/ley-n-21-383-publican-reforma-constitucional-para-establecer-el-desarrollo-cientifico-y-tecnologico-al-servicio-de-las-personas/) |

### 1.8 European Union — AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Full name** | Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 — the EU Artificial Intelligence Act |
| **Date enacted** | In force August 1, 2024; prohibited practices applied February 2, 2025; full application August 2, 2026 |
| **Scope** | EU Regulation (directly binding in 27 member states) |
| **Status** | **Enacted** |
| **Key provisions** | Art. 5(1)(a): Prohibits subliminal manipulation techniques beyond a person's consciousness. | **Limitation** | Not purpose-built for neurorights; "machine-brain interfaces" language appears in Recital 29, not Article 5 text itself. Application to neurotechnology is via general provisions. |

## 4. International Non-Binding Instruments

### 4.1 UNESCO — Recommendation on the Ethics of Neurotechnology (2025)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Full name** | Recommendation on the Ethics of Neurotechnology |
| **Adopted** | November 12, 2025, UNESCO 43rd General Conference |
| **Adopters** | 194 Member States (consensus) |
| **Status** | **Non-binding recommendation** — first global normative framework on neurotechnology |
| **Key provisions** | Full lifecycle coverage (design to disposal). All neural data classified as sensitive. Prohibits tying services to neural data disclosure. Forbids neural data for targeted advertising without consent. Workplace neurotech strictly voluntary/opt-in. Restricts non-therapeutic use on children. Regulatory sandbox mechanisms. |
| **Development** | 8,000+ contributions; expert group chaired by Hervé Chneiweiss (France) and Nita Farahany (US) |
| **URLs** | [UNESCO official](https://www.unesco.org/en/node/86248) | [Process page](https://www.unesco.org/en/ethics-neurotech/recommendation) |

## 9. Landmark Judicial Decisions

### 9.1 Chile — Girardi v. Emotiv (Supreme Court, 2023)

- **Court:** Chilean Supreme Court
- **Ruling:** Rol N° 1.080-2020 (August 2023)
- **Facts:** Former Senator Guido Girardi filed complaint against Emotiv Inc. over neural data collected via consumer EEG "Insight" device
- **Holding:** Neurodata = sensitive biometric data under Data Protection Act 19.698 + constitutional amendment Art. 19. Ordered Emotiv to delete all neural data.
- **Significance:** World's first Supreme Court decision interpreting constitutional neurorights
- **Analysis:** Cornejo-Plaza et al. (2024), *Frontiers in Psychology*. DOI:10.3389/fpsyg.2024.1330439
