1. The Unit Circle: Where Everything Starts

A point moves around a circle of radius 1. Its x-coordinate is cosine. Its y-coordinate is sine. That's it. Every wave, every signal, every Fourier transform starts here.

The red wave trailing to the right is sine: the y-coordinate over time.
The blue wave trailing down is cosine: the x-coordinate over time.

Every wave you've ever seen (sound, light, EEG, radio, ocean) is a combination of these circles. A "complex" signal is just many circles spinning at different speeds, added together.
Deep dive: Why a circle?

A circle is the only shape where the distance from the center never changes but the position keeps moving. That makes it the simplest possible oscillation: constant energy, constantly changing direction.

In physics, this maps to conservation of energy. A pendulum swings because it converts potential energy (height) to kinetic energy (speed) and back. Plot potential vs kinetic and you get a circle. The total (radius) never changes. Only the split between the two rotates.

The unit circle has radius 1, so $\cos^2(\theta) + \sin^2(\theta) = 1$. This is the Pythagorean theorem applied to the circle. The x-component squared plus the y-component squared always equals the radius squared. Total energy is conserved.

2. The Derivative: 90 Degrees Ahead on the Circle

The derivative of sine is cosine. The derivative of cosine is negative sine. On the circle, taking a derivative just means jumping 90 degrees ahead. That's the entire mystery of derivatives for waves.

$\sin \xrightarrow{\frac{d}{dt}} \cos \xrightarrow{\frac{d}{dt}} -\sin \xrightarrow{\frac{d}{dt}} -\cos \xrightarrow{\frac{d}{dt}} \sin$
Take enough derivatives and you end up back where you started. 4 derivatives of sine = sine again. It's a cycle. A circle.

What "derivative" means physically: if sine is where the point is, cosine is how fast it's moving vertically. The derivative always tells you the rate of change, and on a circle, the direction of change is always perpendicular to the current position.
Deep dive: Why 90 degrees?

Imagine you're on a merry-go-round. At any instant, your velocity (the direction you're moving) is tangent to the circle, which is always 90 degrees from the radius (your position vector from center).

Mathematically: the position is $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$. The velocity (derivative) is $(-\sin\theta, \cos\theta)$. Dot product of position and velocity = $-\cos\theta\sin\theta + \sin\theta\cos\theta = 0$. Zero dot product means perpendicular. Always. That's why the derivative is always 90 degrees ahead.

Neuron connection: When a neuron's membrane voltage is at its peak ($dV/dt = 0$), the sodium channels have stopped opening and potassium hasn't fully taken over. For one instant, the system is balanced. The derivative being zero at the peak IS the 90-degree point: position is at max, velocity is zero, about to reverse.

3. Where Derivatives Come From (The Limit)

Before Newton and Leibniz, nobody could answer: "what is the exact speed at a single instant?" You can measure average speed over a time interval. But as the interval shrinks to zero, you get the instantaneous rate of change. That's the derivative.

Use the slider to shrink the interval
$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$
The yellow secant line connects two points on the curve. The slope of that line is the average rate of change over the interval $h$.

As $h$ shrinks toward zero, the secant line rotates and becomes the green tangent line: touching the curve at exactly one point. Its slope is the instantaneous rate of change: the derivative.

That limit process is the entire foundation of calculus. Everything else is consequences.
Deep dive: Why can't you just set h = 0?

If you plug $h = 0$ directly into $\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$, you get $\frac{0}{0}$, which is undefined. You can't divide by zero.

The limit sidesteps this: instead of setting $h = 0$, it asks "what value does the fraction approach as $h$ gets infinitely close to zero?" The answer exists even though $h = 0$ itself doesn't work.

Example: $f(x) = x^2$. The limit becomes $\frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h} = \frac{2xh + h^2}{h} = 2x + h$. As $h \to 0$, this approaches $2x$. So the derivative of $x^2$ is $2x$. The $h$ cancels before we hit zero.

This is why the power rule works: the derivative of $x^n$ is $nx^{n-1}$. The limit calculation always produces a clean cancellation.

Deep dive: What Newton and Leibniz actually fought about

Newton (1666) called them "fluxions" and thought of them as velocities. Leibniz (1684) called them "differentials" and thought of them as infinitely small quantities. Both invented calculus independently. They fought bitterly over priority for decades.

We use Leibniz's notation ($\frac{dy}{dx}$) because it's more intuitive: it looks like a fraction (rise over run), which is exactly what the limit computes. Newton's dot notation ($\dot{y}$) survives mainly in physics for time derivatives.

Neither actually had a rigorous definition of "limit." That came 150 years later from Cauchy and Weierstrass (the epsilon-delta definition). For that entire time, calculus worked perfectly but nobody could explain why it worked. They just knew the answers were right.

4. The Tangent Line: What a Derivative Looks Like

Drag the yellow dot along the curve. The green line is the tangent: it touches the curve at exactly one point and shows the slope (derivative) at that moment. When the curve is rising, the slope is positive. When falling, negative. At the peak, zero.

Drag the yellow dot
The slope number IS the derivative.
At the peak of the wave: slope = 0, derivative = 0 (the wave paused before going back down).
At the steepest rise: slope is maximum positive.
At the steepest fall: slope is maximum negative.

Neuron connection: When a neuron's voltage hits +30mV (peak of action potential), $dV/dt = 0$. The sodium channels have finished opening. For one instant, the voltage isn't changing. Then potassium takes over and it plummets.
Deep dive: Slope as a number

Slope = rise / run. A line going up 3 units for every 1 unit to the right has slope 3. A line going down 2 units per 1 unit right has slope -2. A flat line has slope 0.

The tangent line's slope at any point on $\sin(x)$ equals $\cos(x)$ at that same point. When $x = 0$: $\sin(0) = 0$ (the curve is at zero) and $\cos(0) = 1$ (the slope is 1, rising at 45 degrees). When $x = \pi/2$: $\sin(\pi/2) = 1$ (the peak) and $\cos(\pi/2) = 0$ (slope is flat).

The slope of the slope (second derivative) tells you about curvature: is the curve bending up or bending down? Second derivative of $\sin(x)$ is $-\sin(x)$: negative where the curve is above zero (bending downward), positive where below (bending upward). This is acceleration.

5. The Rules: Power, Chain, Product

Once you understand the limit definition, you can derive rules that let you skip the limit process entirely. These three rules handle 90% of all derivatives you'll ever see.

Deep dive: Why does the power rule work?

Start with the limit definition for $f(x) = x^n$:

$$\frac{(x+h)^n - x^n}{h}$$

Expand $(x+h)^n$ using the binomial theorem: $x^n + nx^{n-1}h + \binom{n}{2}x^{n-2}h^2 + \cdots + h^n$

Subtract $x^n$, divide by $h$: $nx^{n-1} + \text{terms with } h$

As $h \to 0$, all the terms with $h$ vanish, leaving $nx^{n-1}$. The $n$ comes from the binomial coefficient. The exponent drops by 1 because one factor of $x$ was consumed by the $h$ in the denominator.

Visual intuition: The area of a square with side $x$ is $x^2$. If you increase the side by a tiny $h$, the new area is $(x+h)^2 = x^2 + 2xh + h^2$. The change in area is approximately $2xh$ (two thin strips along the sides). Rate of change = $2xh/h = 2x$. The $h^2$ corner is negligibly small.

Deep dive: Chain rule is like gears

If gear A turns gear B, and gear B turns gear C, how fast does C turn relative to A?

$$\frac{dC}{dA} = \frac{dC}{dB} \cdot \frac{dB}{dA}$$

Multiply the individual ratios. If A spins 3x faster than B, and B spins 2x faster than C, then A spins 6x faster than C.

Example: $f(x) = \sin(x^2)$. The outer function is $\sin(\cdot)$, the inner is $x^2$.

$f'(x) = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x$

Derivative of the outer (evaluated at inner) times derivative of the inner. Two gears.

Neuron connection: Ion channel gating works like a chain rule. Voltage changes membrane potential (gear 1). Membrane potential changes channel conformation (gear 2). Channel conformation changes ion flow (gear 3). The total response is the product of all three rates.

6. The Integral: Adding Up the Area

If the derivative asks "how fast is it changing?", the integral asks "how much has accumulated?" Drag the boundary to see the area grow. The integral of sine over a full cycle is zero: positive area and negative area cancel exactly.

Drag to set the boundary
$\int \sin(t)\,dt = -\cos(t) + C$. Integration is the reverse of differentiation. It undoes the derivative.

Why integrals matter for signals:
The energy in a signal is the integral of the signal squared: $\int|f(t)|^2\,dt$
The Fourier transform is an integral: $\int f(t)e^{-i2\pi ft}\,dt$
The total charge moved during an action potential is the integral of current over time.

An action potential's integral must equal zero: every ion that flows in must flow back out. If it didn't, the neuron would accumulate charge and die.
Deep dive: The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The single most important theorem in calculus: differentiation and integration are inverses of each other.

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt = f(x)$$

If you accumulate (integrate) a rate of change, and then ask "how fast is the accumulation changing?" you get back the original rate. It's like asking "I've been driving at varying speeds. My odometer is the integral of my speed. The rate at which my odometer changes IS my speed." Obvious when you say it, but it connects two seemingly different operations (slope-finding and area-finding) into one unified framework.

This is why antiderivatives (reverse differentiation) give you areas. The area under a curve up to point $x$ grows at a rate equal to the curve's height at $x$.

7. Euler's Formula: The Circle Equation

The most beautiful equation in mathematics connects circles, exponentials, and imaginary numbers. A spinning circle IS an exponential function.

$e^{i\theta} = \cos(\theta) + i\sin(\theta)$
One circle = one frequency. Add circles spinning at different speeds and you can draw ANY shape.

This is EXACTLY what a Fourier transform does: it finds the set of circles (frequencies) that, when added together, recreate your signal. An EEG is circles. A voice is circles. A neuron firing is circles.

The QIF coherence metric ($C_s$) measures how orderly these circles are. Coherent brain activity = circles spinning in sync = low entropy. Disrupted brain = circles at random phases = high entropy = low $C_s$.
Deep dive: What is $i$ (the imaginary number)?

$i = \sqrt{-1}$. No real number squared gives -1. So mathematicians invented one and called it "imaginary." Bad name. It's not imaginary. It's rotational.

Multiplying by $i$ rotates a number 90 degrees counterclockwise on the 2D plane. Multiply by $i$ twice ($i^2 = -1$) and you've rotated 180 degrees (flipped sign). Four times ($i^4 = 1$) and you're back where you started. Sound familiar? It's the derivative cycle: $\sin \to \cos \to -\sin \to -\cos \to \sin$.

$i$ is the mathematical encoding of rotation. That's why Euler's formula works: $e^{i\theta}$ is "rotate by angle $\theta$." The exponential function, which normally means growth, becomes rotation when you feed it an imaginary number. Growth and rotation are the same operation in different dimensions.

When $\theta = \pi$: $e^{i\pi} = \cos(\pi) + i\sin(\pi) = -1 + 0 = -1$. So $e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$. Five fundamental constants ($e$, $i$, $\pi$, $1$, $0$) in one equation. Euler's identity.

Deep dive: How Fourier transforms use this

The Fourier transform of a signal $f(t)$ at frequency $\omega$ is:

$$F(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t)\,e^{-i\omega t}\,dt$$

What this actually does: multiply the signal by a spinning circle at frequency $\omega$ ($e^{-i\omega t}$), then add up (integrate) over all time. If the signal has energy at that frequency, the spinning circle and the signal will sync up, and the integral will be large. If not, the positive and negative parts cancel and the integral is small.

It's resonance detection. Each frequency is tested by spinning a circle at that speed and checking: does the signal resonate with this circle? The magnitude of $F(\omega)$ tells you how much.

This is the same principle behind how the cochlea in your ear separates sounds into frequencies, how MRI machines read hydrogen spin echoes, and how the QIF coherence metric decomposes neural signals.

8. E = mc²: Mass is Rotation

Einstein's equation and Euler's formula are saying the same deep thing: one quantity expressed as another quantity transformed. Both are unit conversions between things that seem different but are actually one thing.

$E = mc^2$
Mass IS energy, scaled by $c^2$. They're the same thing measured in different units. A kilogram of matter contains the energy of a 21-megaton nuclear bomb. The $c^2$ is just the conversion factor.
$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$
Exponential growth IS rotation, in the complex plane. Growth and spinning are the same operation in different coordinate systems.
$E = \hbar\omega = hf$
Energy IS frequency. Every quantum particle is a phase rotating at speed $\omega$. More rotation = more energy. A photon isn't "like" a circle. It IS a phase rotating through complex space.
All four are the same statement
$E = mc^2 = \hbar\omega = hf$

Energy = mass = rotation speed = frequency.
The universe computes in circles.
Deep dive: Why Fourier transforms work on everything

Fourier transforms work on everything because everything IS frequencies. Not metaphorically. The math works because reality is built out of rotating phases.

A photon is $e^{i(kx - \omega t)}$. Literally Euler's formula with position and time plugged in. Its energy is proportional to its rotation speed ($E = \hbar\omega$). Slow rotation = radio wave. Fast rotation = gamma ray. Same physics, different $\omega$.

Electrons in atoms don't orbit like planets. They exist as standing waves (circular harmonics) around the nucleus. The "orbitals" in chemistry are solutions to the Schrodinger equation, which is a wave equation. Circles again.

The connection to BCI: Neural oscillations are electromagnetic waves produced by synchronized ion channel activity. When you read an EEG, you're reading the Fourier decomposition of millions of tiny $e^{i\omega t}$ oscillators (neurons). The coherence metric $C_s$ measures how ordered those oscillators are. Seizure = too synchronized (all neurons locked to one frequency). Coma = too desynchronized (no dominant frequency). Healthy cognition = structured balance across multiple frequency bands.

9. Rates of Change: What Derivatives Mean in Life

Position → Velocity → Acceleration
1st derivative of position = velocity (how fast)
2nd derivative of position = acceleration (speeding up or slowing down)
3rd derivative = jerk (why coffee spills when the car lurches)
Voltage → Current → Power change
In a neuron: $dV/dt$ tells you which ion channels are dominating right now. Positive $dV/dt$ = sodium winning (depolarizing). Negative $dV/dt$ = potassium winning (repolarizing).
Population → Growth rate
Exponential growth: the derivative of $e^t$ is $e^t$. The rate of growth equals the current size. This is why pandemics and compound interest feel sudden: the derivative keeps getting bigger.
Dopamine → Learning signal
Reward prediction error (RPE) is a derivative: the difference between expected and actual reward. Your brain computes derivatives to decide whether an outcome was better or worse than predicted.
Red = the function (value over time). Green = its derivative (how fast the value changes). Yellow shading = the integral (total accumulated).

Linear: constant derivative (speed never changes). Integral grows as a triangle.
Quadratic: derivative is a line (speed increases steadily). This is constant acceleration, like gravity.
Exponential: derivative equals the function itself. Growth rate = current value. This is why viral spread feels like it "came out of nowhere."
Sine: derivative is cosine (90 degrees ahead on the circle). We're back to circles.
Deep dive: The dopamine derivative

Your midbrain dopamine neurons fire based on reward prediction error (RPE): the difference between what you expected and what you got.

Better than expected: RPE > 0, dopamine burst, strengthen the neural pathway that led here (learn to repeat this behavior).

Exactly as expected: RPE = 0, no signal, no learning. The pathway is already calibrated.

Worse than expected: RPE < 0, dopamine dip, weaken the pathway (learn to avoid this).

This is a derivative of value over expectation. Your brain doesn't respond to absolute reward. It responds to the rate of change of reward relative to its model. A $10 tip is exciting if you expected $5 (positive derivative). The same $10 is disappointing if you expected $20 (negative derivative). Same value, opposite learning signal.

ADHD medication doesn't change the RPE computation. It ensures the dopamine signal persists long enough in the synapse for the downstream neurons to actually read it and update their weights. The derivative is computed correctly; the signal just evaporates before the receiver can act on it.
