# Ethics Philosophers Through History

A reference guide to the most influential ethics philosophers, organized by tradition and geography. Compiled for autodidactive study.

---

## Western — Ancient & Classical

| Philosopher | Era | Key Works | Core Ideas | Impact on Society |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Socrates** | 470–399 BCE | No written works (preserved by Plato) | The examined life; Socratic method; virtue is knowledge; no one does wrong willingly | Founded Western ethical inquiry. His trial and death became the archetype for intellectual freedom and civil disobedience |
| **Plato** | 428–348 BCE | *Republic*, *Phaedo*, *Symposium*, *Gorgias*, *Meno* | Theory of Forms; justice as harmony of the soul; philosopher-kings; the Good as highest Form | Established the Academy (first university). Shaped political philosophy, education theory, and metaphysical ethics for 2,400 years |
| **Aristotle** | 384–322 BCE | *Nicomachean Ethics*, *Politics*, *Eudemian Ethics* | Virtue ethics; eudaimonia (flourishing); the golden mean; practical wisdom (phronesis) | Defined virtue ethics as a tradition. Influenced medieval Islamic, Jewish, and Christian thought. Still foundational in modern moral philosophy |
| **Epicurus** | 341–270 BCE | *Letter to Menoeceus*, *Principal Doctrines*, *Vatican Sayings* | Pleasure as absence of pain (ataraxia); friendship as highest good; mortality removes fear of death; the gods don't intervene | Offered an ethics of modest living and mental tranquility. Countered superstition. Influenced Enlightenment thinkers (Jefferson, Bentham) |
| **Diogenes of Sinope** | 412–323 BCE | No written works (anecdotal tradition) | Cynicism; live according to nature; reject social conventions; radical honesty and self-sufficiency | Challenged class, materialism, and hypocrisy. Precursor to Stoicism. His provocations remain models of speaking truth to power |

## Western — Stoicism

| Philosopher | Era | Key Works | Core Ideas | Impact on Society |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Zeno of Citium** | 334–262 BCE | *Republic* (lost), fragments | Founded Stoicism; live according to nature; virtue is the sole good; cosmopolitanism | Created the Stoic school. His idea that all humans share rational nature influenced Roman law and later human rights concepts |
| **Cleanthes** | 330–230 BCE | *Hymn to Zeus*, fragments | Divine reason pervades nature; duty and endurance; emotional discipline | Preserved and transmitted early Stoicism. His hymn is one of the earliest monotheistic philosophical texts |
| **Chrysippus** | 279–206 BCE | ~700 works (nearly all lost), fragments | Systematized Stoic logic, physics, and ethics; determinism and fate; assent theory | Called the "second founder" of Stoicism. Built the logical framework that made Stoicism rigorous and transmissible |
| **Hierocles** | 2nd century CE | *Elements of Ethics* (Ἠθικὴ Στοιχείωσις) | Oikeiosis (concentric circles of concern); pull all circles closer to the self; treat strangers as friends, friends as family | His concentric circles model is the earliest systematic framework for expanding moral concern — foundational to cosmopolitanism and modern ethics of care |
| **Seneca** | 4 BCE–65 CE | *Letters to Lucilius*, *On the Shortness of Life*, *On Anger*, *On the Happy Life*, *On Mercy* | Practical Stoicism; emotional mastery; wealth is a tool not a goal; clemency in leadership; death as natural | Brought Stoicism to Roman political life. His letters remain the most accessible entry to Stoic practice. Influenced Montaigne, Descartes, and modern CBT |
| **Epictetus** | 50–135 CE | *Discourses*, *Enchiridion (Handbook)* | Dichotomy of control; freedom through acceptance; we are disturbed not by events but by our judgments; role ethics | Born a slave, became one of history's most influential moral teachers. The dichotomy of control is foundational to CBT and resilience psychology |
| **Marcus Aurelius** | 121–180 CE | *Meditations* | Self-governance; impermanence; duty to the common good; amor fati; the view from above | A Roman emperor writing private reminders to be humble and just. *Meditations* is the most widely read philosophy book by a head of state |
| **Musonius Rufus** | 30–101 CE | *Lectures and Fragments* | Equal education for women; marriage as partnership; simple living; philosophy as practice not theory | Called the "Roman Socrates." One of the earliest advocates for gender equality in education. Teacher of Epictetus |

## Western — Medieval & Renaissance

| Philosopher | Era | Key Works | Core Ideas | Impact on Society |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Augustine of Hippo** | 354–430 CE | *Confessions*, *City of God*, *On Free Choice of the Will* | Original sin; divine grace; free will vs. predestination; just war theory; the two cities (earthly/heavenly) | Shaped Christian ethics for 1,500 years. Just war theory still governs international law. Confessions invented the autobiography as moral inquiry |
| **Thomas Aquinas** | 1225–1274 | *Summa Theologica*, *Summa Contra Gentiles* | Natural law; synthesis of Aristotle and Christianity; the five ways; virtue and divine law; double effect | Natural law theory underpins Catholic moral theology and influenced the UN Declaration of Human Rights. Doctrine of double effect still used in medical and military ethics |
| **Maimonides** | 1138–1204 | *Guide for the Perplexed*, *Mishneh Torah* | Synthesis of Jewish law and Aristotelian philosophy; negative theology; eight levels of charity; intellectual virtue as highest | Codified Jewish ethics. His charity ladder remains the standard framework for philanthropic giving |
| **Niccolò Machiavelli** | 1469–1527 | *The Prince*, *Discourses on Livy* | Political realism; ends justify means (for state stability); virtù vs. fortuna; separation of politics from morality | Invented modern political science. Forced ethics to confront power as it actually operates, not as it should |
| **Thomas More** | 1478–1535 | *Utopia* | Critique of private property; communal living; religious tolerance; social justice through institutional design | Coined "utopia." Established the tradition of imagining better societies as ethical argument. Died for conscience over power |

## Western — Enlightenment & Modern

|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Thomas Hobbes** | 1588–1679 | *Leviathan*, *De Cive* | Social contract; state of nature ("nasty, brutish, and short"); sovereign authority as escape from chaos; rational self-interest | Founded modern political philosophy. | **John Locke** | 1632–1704 | *Two Treatises of Government*, *Essay Concerning Human Understanding*, *A Letter Concerning Toleration* | Natural rights (life, liberty, property); government by consent; tabula rasa; religious toleration | Direct influence on the US Declaration of Independence and Constitution. Father of liberalism |
| **Baruch Spinoza** | 1632–1677 | *Ethics*, *Theologico-Political Treatise* | God and nature are one substance; freedom through understanding necessity; emotions as confused ideas; democratic governance | Laid groundwork for secular ethics, biblical criticism, and liberal democracy. Einstein cited him as his philosophical model |
| **David Hume** | 1711–1776 | *A Treatise of Human Nature*, *An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* | Is-ought problem; moral sentiment over reason; skepticism of causation; sympathy as basis of morality | Showed that morality cannot be derived from facts alone. Woke Kant from his "dogmatic slumber." Foundation of empirical ethics |
| **Jean-Jacques Rousseau** | 1712–1778 | *The Social Contract*, *Emile*, *Discourse on Inequality* | General will; humans are naturally good but corrupted by society; freedom through civic participation; education as moral development | Inspired the French Revolution. His educational philosophy reshaped childhood and pedagogy. Influenced Romanticism |
| **Immanuel Kant** | 1724–1804 | *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*, *Critique of Practical Reason*, *Metaphysics of Morals* | Categorical imperative; duty-based ethics (deontology); treat humanity never merely as means; moral law from reason alone; autonomy | Defined deontological ethics. The categorical imperative remains the primary alternative to consequentialism. Shaped human rights and international law |
| **Mary Wollstonecraft** | 1759–1797 | *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, *A Vindication of the Rights of Men* | Women are rational beings deserving equal education; virtue has no sex; critique of aristocratic privilege; independence through reason | Founded feminist ethics 70 years before Mill. Argued women's subordination was not natural but produced by lack of education. Influenced every suffrage and women's rights movement that followed |
| **Adam Smith** | 1723–1790 | *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, *The Wealth of Nations* | Moral sentiment and sympathy; the impartial spectator; self-interest channeled by moral constraints; justice as foundation of society | *Theory of Moral Sentiments* is a foundational ethics text, not just economics. Showed that markets require moral foundations. The "impartial spectator" anticipated modern theories of moral reasoning |
| **Arthur Schopenhauer** | 1788–1860 | *The World as Will and Representation*, *On the Basis of Morality* | Will as blind striving; compassion (Mitleid) as basis of morality; denial of the will; suffering as universal condition; Eastern-Western synthesis | First major Western philosopher to engage seriously with Hindu and Buddhist thought. His compassion-based ethics directly influenced Nietzsche, Wittgenstein, and Tolstoy. Key bridge between Eastern and Western moral philosophy |
| **Jeremy Bentham** | 1748–1832 | *An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation*, *The Panopticon* | Utilitarianism; greatest happiness for greatest number; hedonic calculus; animal rights ("Can they suffer?"); prison reform | Founded utilitarianism. First major philosopher to argue for animal rights and decriminalization of homosexuality. Shaped modern policy analysis |
| **John Stuart Mill** | 1806–1873 | *Utilitarianism*, *On Liberty*, *The Subjection of Women* | Refined utilitarianism (higher/lower pleasures); harm principle; liberty of thought and expression; women's equality | *On Liberty* is the foundational text for free speech. Championed women's suffrage in Parliament. Made utilitarianism workable |
| **Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel** | 1770–1831 | *Phenomenology of Spirit*, *Philosophy of Right* | Dialectics (thesis-antithesis-synthesis); ethical life (Sittlichkeit); history as progress of freedom; recognition | His dialectic shaped Marx, existentialism, and critical theory. Ethical life concept bridges individual morality and social institutions |
| **Friedrich Nietzsche** | 1844–1900 | *Beyond Good and Evil*, *On the Genealogy of Morals*, *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, *The Gay Science* | Master-slave morality; will to power; eternal recurrence; death of God; creation of values; amor fati | Dismantled inherited moral frameworks. Forced philosophy to confront nihilism. Influenced existentialism, postmodernism, and psychology |
| **Søren Kierkegaard** | 1813–1855 | *Either/Or*, *Fear and Trembling*, *The Sickness Unto Death* | Three stages of existence (aesthetic, ethical, religious); leap of faith; anxiety and despair as ethical signals; subjective truth | Father of existentialism. Showed that ethics requires personal commitment, not just rational principles |

| Philosopher | Era | Key Works | Core Ideas | Impact on Society |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hannah Arendt** | 1906–1975 | *The Human Condition*, *Eichmann in Jerusalem*, *The Origins of Totalitarianism* | Banality of evil; public vs. private realm; natality (capacity to begin); totalitarianism as destruction of moral thinking | "Banality of evil" changed how we understand moral failure. Essential reading for genocide prevention and political ethics |

| **Emmanuel Levinas** | 1906–1995 | *Totality and Infinity*, *Otherwise than Being* | Ethics as first philosophy; the face of the Other; infinite responsibility; ethics precedes ontology | Recentered philosophy on responsibility to others. ## Eastern — Chinese Tradition

| **Confucius (Kong Qiu)** | 551–479 BCE | *Analects* (論語, compiled by disciples) | Ren (benevolence/humaneness); li (ritual propriety); filial piety; junzi (exemplary person); rectification of names | Defined Chinese civilization for 2,500 years. Defined orthodoxy across East Asia |

## The Common Thread — How It All Ties Together

Ethics has been a global conversation for over 4,000 years. The thinkers above span five continents, dozens of languages, and wildly different political realities. Yet when you step back and look at what they all kept circling back to, the overlap is striking.

### The Universal Principles

Six principles show up across virtually every tradition listed here, independently discovered by people who never read each other's work:

1. **Treat others as moral equals.** Confucius called it ren (humaneness). Kant called it the categorical imperative. Ubuntu says "I am because we are." Mozi demanded universal love. The Golden Rule appears in some form in every tradition on this page. The specific framing changes; the core demand does not.

2. **Character is built through practice.** Aristotle said virtue comes from habituation. Confucius said it comes through ritual. The Stoics used daily exercises. Dogen said zazen is enlightenment itself, not a path to it. Al-Ghazali prescribed spiritual exercises. Nobody on this list believed you could think your way to being a good person without actually doing things differently.

3. **Justice requires more than good intentions.** From Ptahhotep's maxims for Egyptian rulers to Rawls's veil of ignorance, every tradition recognized that personal virtue is not enough. Institutions, laws, and governance structures must be designed to produce fair outcomes. Chanakya wrote a manual for it. Plato imagined a whole republic around it. Han Fei said forget virtue entirely and design better incentive systems.

4. **Expand your circle of concern.** Hierocles drew literal concentric circles and said: pull them all closer. The Buddha extended compassion to all sentient beings. Singer calls it the expanding moral circle. Rumi dissolved the boundaries entirely. The direction of ethical progress, across every tradition, has been outward: from self to family to community to strangers to other species.

5. **Know yourself honestly.** Socrates made it his motto. Laozi said return to simplicity. Kierkegaard said truth is subjective and requires personal commitment. Fanon showed that colonial power distorts self-knowledge. Shankara said the self is identical with ultimate reality. The starting point is always the same: you cannot act well if you do not see clearly, and seeing clearly starts with yourself.

6. **Power must be constrained.** Machiavelli described power as it actually works. Hobbes said unconstrained power produces chaos. Locke said government rules only by consent. Foucault showed how power operates through institutions most people never question. Arendt showed what happens when moral thinking collapses entirely. Every tradition produced thinkers who warned: power without accountability destroys the people it claims to serve.

### The Four-Stage Evolution

Ethics did not stay in one place. Across traditions, it evolved through roughly four stages, though different regions moved through them at different speeds and in different orders:

**Stage 1: Individual Virtue.** The earliest ethics asked: what kind of person should I be? Ptahhotep, Confucius, Socrates, the Buddha, Zarathustra — they all started here. Be just. Be courageous. Be compassionate. Be honest. The unit of analysis was the individual character.

**Stage 2: Social Contract.** As societies grew more complex, the question shifted: how should we organize ourselves? Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Mozi, Han Fei, and Chanakya all asked what agreements make collective life possible. Rights, laws, and mutual obligations entered the picture. Ethics became political.

**Stage 3: Institutional Justice.** By the modern era, thinkers recognized that individual virtue and social agreements were not enough. Rawls asked what institutions a rational person would design from behind a veil of ignorance. Nussbaum asked what capabilities every human needs to flourish. Habermas asked how democratic institutions can be legitimate. The focus moved from personal character and agreements to structural fairness.

**Stage 4: Global Responsibility.** The most recent stage asks: what do we owe to people we will never meet? Singer argues we have obligations to distant strangers and future generations. Fanon showed that colonial structures create global injustice. The Ubuntu tradition insists that personhood is relational across all of humanity. Ethics now operates at planetary scale, grappling with climate, technology, and systemic inequality.

These stages did not replace each other. Virtue still matters. Social contracts still matter. But the scope kept expanding. The trend line across 4,000 years of ethical thought, from Egypt to Greece to China to India to Africa to the modern West, points in one direction: toward wider inclusion, deeper accountability, and a harder look at power.

The thinkers on this page disagreed about nearly everything specific. They disagreed about God, human nature, the role of emotion, the source of moral authority, and whether violence is ever justified. But they agreed, across millennia and oceans, that human beings owe each other something — and that figuring out what that something is remains the most important work there is.
