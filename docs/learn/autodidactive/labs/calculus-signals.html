Calculus of Neural Signals

How derivatives, integrals, and Fourier transforms describe your brain

1. What is a derivative? (The Ball)

A derivative measures how fast something is changing. Not how much. How fast. Click and drag the ball to see its position, velocity (first derivative), and acceleration (second derivative) in real time.

Drag the ball along the hill
Position = where the ball is (the value)
Velocity = how fast it's moving (first derivative, dx/dt)
Acceleration = how fast the velocity is changing (second derivative, d^2x/dt^2)

When the ball is at the top of a hill, velocity = 0 (it stopped climbing). That's a local maximum. Calculus finds these by setting the derivative to zero.

Key moments:
t = 0.5ms: Derivative goes positive (voltage starts rising, Na+ channels opening)
t = 1.0ms: Derivative is at MAXIMUM (fastest rise, positive feedback loop)
t = 1.3ms: Derivative = 0 (voltage peaked at +30mV, dV/dt = 0 means local max)

t = 2.5ms: Everything returns to baseline

The yellow shaded area is the integral: total charge moved across the membrane. This must balance to zero (what goes in must come out), which is why there's a hyperpolarization dip after the spike.

3. Waves, Frequency, and Your EEG

Your brain produces waves at different frequencies. Adjust the sliders to mix them and see how complex signals are built from simple sine waves. This is what Fourier transforms decompose.

Each band = a sine wave at a different frequency.
Your EEG is the sum of all of them. The Fourier transform (an integral: \u222Bf(t)e^{-i2\u03C0ft}dt) pulls them apart, telling you how much power is at each frequency. This is how the coherence metric Cs in QIF works: it measures whether these frequency components are stable and ordered, or noisy and chaotic.

4. Derivatives as Edge Detection

Your brain uses derivatives constantly. Every time you detect a change (a sound starting, a light flickering, a touch beginning), your neurons are computing the derivative of the input. Constant inputs fade (habituation). Changes pop out. Draw a signal and see its derivative.

Your signal
Derivative (what neurons detect)
Try drawing:
A sharp step: big derivative spike (your brain notices sudden changes)
A gradual slope: small constant derivative (you habituate to slow changes)
A flat line then spike: derivative is zero, then huge (this is why a sudden sound startles you)

This is why the boiling frog attack in Neurowall evades detection. The signal drifts so slowly that the derivative never exceeds the detection threshold. AC coupling (subtracting the mean) is mathematically the same as ignoring the zero-frequency component of the Fourier transform.

5. L = v/f (The QIF Equation)

Conduction velocity divided by frequency gives you the spatial wavelength: how far a signal travels in one oscillation cycle. Adjust the sliders to see how myelination (velocity) and brainwave frequency interact.

What this means:
A myelinated axon at v = 50 m/s carrying a 10 Hz alpha wave has a wavelength of L = 5 meters. The wave repeats every 5 meters along the axon.

Demyelination (from B12 deficiency, MS) drops v. Same frequency, shorter wavelength. Signals from different brain regions arrive out of phase. That's desynchronization, and it's what makes thinking feel fragmented.

Calculus connection: Velocity IS a derivative (v = dx/dt). Frequency is the derivative of phase (f = d\u03C6/dt / 2\u03C0). L = v/f is a relationship between two derivatives.
