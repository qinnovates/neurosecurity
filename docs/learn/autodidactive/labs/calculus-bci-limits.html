1. The Thermal Ceiling: Power as a Derivative

An implant dissipates heat. Brain tissue can only absorb 1.0 °C of temperature rise before damage begins (IEC 60601-1). Power is a rate: energy per second. Temperature rise is the integral of that power over time, minus what blood flow (perfusion) carries away.

$\Delta T = \frac{P_{\text{total}}}{4\pi k r} \cdot \left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right) \leq 1.0°C$
Power ($P$) is a derivative: $P = dE/dt$ (energy change per second).
Temperature ($\Delta T$) is the integral: how much heat has accumulated minus what perfusion removes.
Perfusion is nature's cooling system. More blood flow = more heat carried away.

The constraint: No matter how many channels you want, total power dissipation must stay under the thermal ceiling. Neuralink N1: 1024 channels at ~23 mW. The limit for cortical surface is ~40 mW. Go over and you cook the tissue.
Deep dive: Why power scales with channels

Each recording channel needs an amplifier, ADC, and digital logic. Modern neural amplifiers consume ~1-5 $\mu$W per channel. So 1000 channels = 1-5 mW just for analog front-end. Add digital processing, wireless telemetry, and stimulation, and total power climbs to 15-40 mW.

The derivative relationship: $P_{\text{total}} = n_{\text{ch}} \cdot P_{\text{per\_ch}} + P_{\text{digital}} + P_{\text{telemetry}}$

As $n_{\text{ch}}$ grows (Moore's Law doubling every ~7.4 years), $P_{\text{per\_ch}}$ must shrink at least as fast, or the thermal ceiling is hit. This is why BCI scaling is fundamentally a power engineering problem.

2. Signal vs Noise: The Thermal Floor

Neural spikes are tiny: 40-500 $\mu$V. The electrode and tissue generate thermal noise (Johnson-Nyquist noise) that sets an absolute floor on what you can detect. This floor comes directly from the Boltzmann constant and body temperature.

$V_{\text{noise}} = \sqrt{4 k_B T R \Delta f} \quad \approx 13.1\;\mu V \text{ at } 1\text{M}\Omega, 10\text{kHz}, 310\text{K}$
This is thermodynamics meeting calculus. The noise voltage comes from the square root of four quantities multiplied together:
$k_B$ = Boltzmann constant (how much energy per degree of temperature)
$T$ = body temperature (310 K)
$R$ = electrode impedance (resistance)
$\Delta f$ = measurement bandwidth

The signal-to-noise ratio ($\text{SNR} = V_{\text{spike}} / V_{\text{noise}}$) determines if you can even see a neural spike. Below SNR = 3, spikes are buried in noise. This is a physics limit, not an engineering one.
Deep dive: Where the noise comes from (circles again)

Johnson-Nyquist noise is caused by thermal agitation of electrons in the electrode. At body temperature, electrons have kinetic energy $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$ per degree of freedom. They bounce around randomly, creating tiny random voltages.

The Fourier transform of this random signal is flat across all frequencies (white noise). Each frequency bin contributes equally. The total noise power is the integral over the measurement bandwidth: $\int_0^{\Delta f} 4k_BTR\,df = 4k_BTR\Delta f$.

This is why bandwidth matters: wider bandwidth = integrating more noise frequencies = more total noise. Neural spike detection uses 300-10,000 Hz. If you could narrow that, you'd reduce noise, but you'd also lose the spike waveform (which has frequency content across that whole range).

The circle connection: White noise is ALL circles (all frequencies) at equal amplitude with random phases. A neural spike is a specific pattern of circles. Detection = finding the spike's pattern in a sea of random circles. SNR tells you how loud the spike's circles are relative to the noise circles.

3. Shannon Capacity: The Information Limit

Claude Shannon proved in 1948 that every noisy channel has a maximum data rate. No matter how clever your encoding, you cannot exceed this limit. For BCI, this sets the theoretical maximum bits per second you can extract from neural tissue.

$C = B \cdot \log_2(1 + \text{SNR})$
Bandwidth ($B$) is a frequency range (the set of circles your channel can carry).
SNR determines how many distinguishable signal levels fit above the noise floor.
$\log_2$ converts signal levels to bits.

A 10 kHz bandwidth with SNR of 15 dB gives a capacity of ~40 kbits/sec per channel. With 1024 channels, theoretical max is ~40 Mbits/sec. But wireless telemetry from inside the skull to outside is the bottleneck: current BCI links manage ~1-20 Mbps total.
Deep dive: Logarithms are inverse exponentials

$\log_2(x)$ asks: "2 raised to what power gives $x$?" It's the inverse of $2^x$.

Shannon used $\log_2$ because information is measured in bits (binary digits). Each bit doubles the number of distinguishable messages. 1 bit = 2 messages, 2 bits = 4, 10 bits = 1024. The log tells you how many doublings you have available.

Why SNR is inside a log: Adding more signal power gives diminishing returns. Going from SNR 10 to 20 (doubling power) doesn't double capacity. It adds $\log_2(21) - \log_2(11) \approx 0.93$ bits per Hertz. The log compresses the relationship. This is fundamental: information is logarithmic in signal power.

Calculus connection: The derivative of $\ln(x)$ is $1/x$. Each additional unit of signal power contributes less and less to capacity. This is the mathematical reason that brute-force power increases can't overcome fundamental limits.

4. Channel Scaling: Exponential Growth Meets Physics Walls

BCI channel counts double every ~7.4 years (Stevenson & Kording, 2011). This is exponential growth. The derivative of an exponential is itself: the rate of channel growth is proportional to how many channels you already have. But exponentials always hit walls.

$n_{\text{ch}}(t) = n_0 \cdot 2^{t / T_{\text{double}}}$
Red curve: unconstrained exponential growth (what happens if physics doesn't intervene).
Yellow ceiling: the thermal limit (max channels before $\Delta T > 1°C$).
Green curve: actual trajectory (exponential that saturates at the ceiling).

Calculus connection: The derivative of $n_0 \cdot 2^{t/T}$ is $\frac{n_0 \ln 2}{T} \cdot 2^{t/T}$. The growth rate itself grows exponentially. This is why exponentials feel slow at first, then explode. Neuralink went from 96 to 1024 channels (10x) in 5 years. The next 10x (to 10,000) will run into the thermal ceiling unless power per channel drops proportionally.

5. Coherence ($C_s$): The Signal Integrity Constraint

Even if you have enough channels, enough bandwidth, and stay under the thermal ceiling, the BCI must not disrupt the brain's own signal coherence. $C_s = e^{-S_c}$ where $S_c$ is spectral entropy. This is the QIF security constraint: the one nobody else measures.

$C_s = e^{-S_c} \geq C_{s,\min}(F)$
Blue spectrum: the frequency decomposition of the neural signal (Fourier transform).
Green line: $C_s$ value (coherence). High when spectrum is ordered, low when noisy.
Red: attack injection adds noise or foreign frequencies, disrupting the spectrum.

$e^{-S_c}$ is a decaying exponential. As entropy ($S_c$) increases, coherence drops toward zero. The derivative of $e^{-x}$ is $-e^{-x}$: the rate of coherence loss is proportional to the current coherence level. Small disruptions to a coherent signal cause small drops. But once coherence is already low, even more disruption barely changes it (the signal is already destroyed).
Deep dive: Why $e^{-x}$ and not some other decay

The exponential $e^{-x}$ is the unique function where the rate of decay equals the current value. This models systems where degradation is proportional to what remains: radioactive decay, capacitor discharge, and neural signal coherence.

Spectral entropy $S_c = -\sum p_i \log p_i$ where $p_i$ is the power fraction at each frequency bin. A perfectly ordered signal (single frequency) has $S_c = 0$, giving $C_s = 1$. A flat (white noise) spectrum maximizes $S_c$, driving $C_s$ toward 0.

The $C_s$ threshold depends on what brain function ($F$) you're trying to preserve. Motor control might tolerate $C_s \geq 0.3$. Cognitive tasks need $C_s \geq 0.7$. Consciousness itself may require $C_s \geq 0.5$ (this is speculative, connects to IIT).

All 13 constraints are coupled. You can't solve one without affecting others. This visualization shows the feasible region: the set of (channels, power, bandwidth) combinations where ALL constraints are satisfied simultaneously.

The key insight: Every constraint is calculus. Thermal limits are integrals (accumulated heat). Noise floors are square roots of integrals (total noise power across bandwidth). Shannon capacity uses logarithms (inverse exponentials). Channel scaling is exponential growth. Coherence is exponential decay. The BCI Limits Equation is a system of coupled differential and integral constraints.

The feasible design region shrinks over time as channel counts grow (exponential pressure against thermal ceiling), but expands as power-per-channel drops (engineering progress). The question is which rate wins. So far, engineering is keeping up. But the thermal ceiling is absolute.
